About me

My fascination with technology has always been driven by a desire to understand how things work. Quite early on, this lead to me to experiment with electronics and little arduino projects. But just as much as I enjoyed technology, I was drawn to visual arts, cinema and music. I’ve played and instrument for the majority of my life and I’ve always been fascinated by movies and filmmaking. During my highschool years, I gradually switched from moving image to photography and spent much of my free time exploring Munich with a camera. I also developed a lasting passion for analog photography, and started experimenting with different processes and printing techniques.

After graduating, I was torn between pursuing my artistic interests or taking a more technical path. I eventually decided to strike a balance between the two by studying Computer Science in a dual studies program with cinema camera manufacturer ARRI. Though I never really considered a degree in “pure” computer science, I ended up really enjoying the deep understanding it gave me about mathematical and theoretical concepts — especially when subjects intersected with the “real world”, like in signal theory, mathematical modeling or the AICA project workshop.

At ARRI, I had the chance to go through several departments, including Camera Service, Project- / Quality Management, and Camera R&D, before joining the then newly formed Solutions department. This team focused on developing new production workflows in response to the rapid technological advancements in the industry. The small initiative operated much like an experimental R&D Lab, and I really enjoyed its hands-on prototyping approach, especially compared to the more traditionally structured departments I had worked in before.

My main responsibility within Solutions was developing a software application for color calibration in virtual production studios. The goal was to create a semi-automated workflow by integrating camera communication through an IP interface, enabling automatic setting adjustments and image captures. While my initial role was UI development, I gradually took on additional tasks, including implementing the IP interface, refining the backend calibration algorithm, and deploying the final application. The application was originally only compatible with ARRI cameras, but I later expanded the workflow into a universal calibration system as part of my bachelor's thesis.

My Interests

For a long time, I wasn’t quite sure where I fit between art and science. While choosing a more technical path for my bachelor’s degree ultimately felt like the right decision, I often found myself envious of the things my creative peers would do, feeling like I’m wasting my time on mathematics and theory. At some point this changed, and I started two realize (1) what an amazing toolset my IT studies gave me to pursue creative work and (2) how exciting it is when the two worlds overlap. Once I leaned into that intersection, I discovered so many fascinating applications and met more and more people who shared the same combined passion.

Looking back, I think one reason I struggled to find my place was because I kept thinking in terms of fixed disciplines rather than interests. I’ve always loved problem-solving and designing experiences, but that interest has never been limited to programming. Same goes for my artistic work, which has always revolved around human interactions and visual patterns, even if the medium I use to express them keeps shifting. This non-disciplinary way of thinking was a real revelation for me, and also made me realize how similar the two fields really are.

Recently, I also started exploring theoretical perspectives on the subject, like the wonderfuI book “The new landscape in art and science” by Gyorgy Kepes, who argues that the two fields are inherently interconnected. Kepes emphasizes the importance of interdisciplinary work, considering the “new landscape” of a world shaped by technology and science. Even though he wrote this book in the 1950s, long before many of the innovations that define our current society, his ideas feel more relevant than ever.

Another concept I often revisit is Kurzweils theory of the “technological singularity”, which I covered in a thesis a few years ago. He proposes that the speed of all human innovation accelerates exponentially, leading to a point of infinitely rapid development in the near future — driven by AI and eventually AGI. Even though I would nowadays consider his approach too technology-driven and idealistic, the general model feels quite fitting. It’s a reminder of how urgently we need to think not only about what’s possible, but about what’s right.

Selected Work
2025
Bachelor Thesis
Research / Dev

As a follow-up to my work in the Solutions Department at ARRI, my bachelor’s thesis also focused on color calibration for Virtual Production studios. Based on an existing proprietary approach and additional scientific research, I developed a camera-agnostic workflow that enables calibration across a wide range of camera setups and system configurations. Next to the calibration algorithm, I also implemented a verification workflow, designed an accompanying user interface, and conducted extensive testing across diverse camera models and production conditions to ensure robustness and accuracy. I really enjoyed working with color because it combines quite a complex scientific problem with simple perceptual intuition. You can immediately tell when an image is calibrated correctly because it just looks pleasing, but at the same time, there’s an underlying mathematical framework that explains exactly why.

Find the entire thesis here.

2025
AICA Project Workshop
Design / Dev

In the final semester of my bachelor’s degree, I took part in the AICA project workshop — an interdisciplinary initiative led by multiple Munich universities to address challenges at the intersection of AI, culture, and the arts. In a team of four, we developed an interactive art installation exploring feedback loops in generative AI models, paying homage to a musical piece by Alvin Lucier. I was mainly responsible for the technical development, from setting up a local StableDiffusion model and designing prompts to writing the control script and building the hardware interface using an ESP32. Despite the tight timeframe, this project showed me how rewarding it is to collaborate with people from different disciplines — and unexpectedly, it also taught me a lot about woodworking and processing metal.

Find the GitHub repo here.

2024
Personal Website
Design / Dev

Although my photographic journey originally started on Instagram, I found myself losing interest in the platform in recent years. At the same time, I’ve always enjoyed exploring user interaction and rethinking interfaces. So, I eventually decided to create my own personal website to showcase my photography. I wanted to keep the aspects I liked about social media (image grid, chronological feed, minimal metadata) while removing all the clutter around it. Inspired by archival projects and novel content platforms like Are.na, I ended up with a very minimal, image-centric layout, which I implemented from scratch using PHP and a basic folder-based CMS solution.

Find the site here.

2023-now
Träumen
photography

Träumen is an unfinished photography project about the fascination I have with memory and dream images. The original idea was to visualize these concepts using photography, capturing everyday moments that evoke a sense of surrealism, as if they were part of a dream. On a less metaphorical level, it became a way for me to disconnect during stressful times - grabbing my camera and following wherever my intuition led me. My plan is to eventually turn these into a printed zine, I just haven’t found the time to do it.

Why D&C

I often come back to a thesis I wrote in highschool about Kurzweils theory of the technological singularity. He proposes that the speed of all human innovation accelerates exponentially, leading to a point of infinitely rapid development in the near future — driven by AI and eventually AGI. Even though I would nowadays consider his approach too technology-driven and idealistic, the general model feels quite fitting in a world where innovation already seems to outpace our ability to process it. I see it as a reminder of how urgently we need to think not only about what’s possible, but about what’s right.

Therefore, I would love to explore these questions in a collaborative, interdisciplinary environment like the Design&Computation Master. I've been following the program for a while now, and I'm constantly amazed by the project output. I also really loved the spirit I experienced when visiting for the Info Event and left feeling genuinely excited about the chance to work with such a diverse and skilled group.

As established before, I really believe in the interdisciplinary approach and I think my technical knowledge and creative background would be a nice addition to the team.
